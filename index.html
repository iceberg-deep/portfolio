# About

I was raised in the SOC. Four years in the blue dark, watching the boards light up at 3 a.m., triaging alerts nobody else wanted, walking the wreckage of other people's incidents, and reading the story the malware left behind. I learned the glass from the inside. Which alerts scream and which ones die quiet in a queue nobody reads, where the sensors go blind, where the logs just stop.

Now I'm on the other side of the mirror.

Junior on offense, and I won't pretend otherwise. But I carry something most of the fresh ones don't. I remember being the one who was supposed to catch me. Every move I make, there's a ghost of an analyst at my shoulder asking, "Will this trip the wire?" And most of the time I already know the answer, because that used to be my wire.

Scope to execution to findings to the slow bleed of remediation after. I've walked the whole arc.

And when the tooling gets in my way, I stop asking it for permission. I build my own.

That instinct is not new. I have been writing code since Visual Basic 6, living in terminals since MSDOS and Windows 3.1, back when you reached America Online through the screech of a 56k modem and files still lived on the B drive. I built my first PC out of parts I scavenged from a tech company that was going under in the dot-com crash. The résumé says junior on offense. The hands have been in the machine most of my life.
